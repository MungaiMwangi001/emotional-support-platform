Sleep and Mental Health

Sleep is one of the most important factors affecting mental health, yet it's often one of the first things students sacrifice under pressure. Understanding the relationship between sleep and mental health can motivate healthier sleep habits.

Why Sleep Matters for Mental Health
During sleep, your brain consolidates memories, processes emotions, clears waste products, and restores itself. Insufficient sleep impairs emotional regulation, increases anxiety and irritability, reduces stress resilience, impairs concentration and memory, and increases risk of depression.

Most adults need 7-9 hours of sleep per night. Sleep deprivation is cumulative—you can't fully recover from a week of poor sleep in one night.

Common Sleep Challenges for Students
- Irregular schedules due to class times, social activities, and work
- Caffeine consumption, especially later in the day
- Screen use before bed (blue light suppresses melatonin)
- Anxiety and racing thoughts at bedtime
- Inconsistent wake times on weekends disrupting circadian rhythm

Evidence-Based Sleep Improvement Strategies

Sleep Hygiene
Keep consistent sleep and wake times (even on weekends). Create a cool, dark, quiet sleep environment. Reserve the bedroom primarily for sleep—avoid studying in bed. Develop a relaxing pre-sleep routine.

Managing Pre-Sleep Anxiety
If racing thoughts keep you awake, try writing them down before bed to "offload" them from your mind. Progressive muscle relaxation or deep breathing can also help quiet an active mind.

Limiting Screen Use
Blue light from screens delays melatonin release. Try to stop using screens 30-60 minutes before bed, or use blue light filtering settings if screen use is unavoidable.

Addressing Insomnia
If you've been lying awake for 20+ minutes, get up and do something calm in dim light until you feel sleepy. This prevents associating the bed with wakefulness.

When Poor Sleep Persists
If sleep problems persist despite good sleep hygiene, or if you're experiencing excessive daytime sleepiness, consider speaking with a healthcare provider. Sleep disorders like insomnia or sleep apnea are treatable conditions.
