Supporting Yourself Through Difficult Times

It's normal to feel sad, low, or discouraged sometimes, especially during challenging periods like exams, transitions, or personal difficulties. However, when these feelings persist, it may be a sign that you need additional support.

Recognizing When You Need Support
Signs that you may benefit from additional support include: persistent sadness or emptiness lasting more than two weeks, loss of interest in activities you usually enjoy, significant changes in sleep or appetite, difficulty concentrating, fatigue or low energy, feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt, and thoughts of death or suicide.

If you're experiencing thoughts of self-harm or suicide, please reach out to a mental health professional or crisis line immediately.

Self-Care Strategies

Behavioral Activation
When feeling low, motivation often disappears. The key is to act before you feel like it. Start small: even a 10-minute walk, calling a friend, or completing one small task can shift your mood and build momentum.

Maintaining Routines
Structure provides stability during difficult times. Try to maintain regular sleep, meal times, and daily activities even when it feels difficult. Consistent routines help regulate mood and energy.

Social Connection
Depression often makes people withdraw from others, which tends to worsen symptoms. Gentle reconnection, even when it feels difficult, can provide significant relief. You don't have to talk about your struggles; just being around others can help.

Self-Compassion
Treat yourself with the kindness you would show a good friend. When you notice self-critical thoughts, pause and ask: "Would I say this to someone I care about?" Practice speaking to yourself with warmth and understanding.

Meaningful Activities
Engaging in activities that provide meaning, purpose, or accomplishment—even small ones—can counteract feelings of hopelessness. This might include creative projects, helping others, learning something new, or spending time in nature.

Getting Professional Help
Therapy (particularly CBT, behavioral activation, or interpersonal therapy) and sometimes medication are highly effective for depression. Your campus counseling center is a great first step. Don't wait until things get worse—early intervention is most effective.
