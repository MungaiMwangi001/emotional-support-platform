Mindfulness and Self-Care Practices for Students

Mindfulness is the practice of paying attention to the present moment with curiosity and without judgment. Research consistently shows that mindfulness reduces stress, anxiety, and depression while improving focus, emotional regulation, and overall wellbeing.

What is Mindfulness?
Mindfulness means being fully aware of what you're experiencing in the present moment – your thoughts, feelings, bodily sensations, and surrounding environment – without trying to change or judge it. It's about observing your inner experience rather than being swept away by it.

Simple Mindfulness Exercises

Body Scan Meditation: Lie or sit comfortably. Slowly move your attention through different parts of your body, from your toes to the top of your head. Notice any sensations – tension, warmth, tingling – without trying to change them. This can be done in 5-20 minutes.

Mindful Breathing: Sit comfortably and close your eyes. Focus all your attention on your breath. Notice the sensation of air entering and leaving your nostrils, your chest rising and falling. When your mind wanders (it will), gently bring it back to your breath without self-criticism.

5-Senses Exercise: Pause and notice: 5 things you can see, 4 things you can physically feel, 3 things you can hear, 2 things you can smell, 1 thing you can taste. This grounds you in the present moment.

Mindful Walking: Walk at a slow, natural pace. Pay attention to the sensations of walking – the feeling of your feet touching the ground, the movement of your legs, the rhythm of your breath. When your mind wanders, return attention to the physical sensations.

Mindful Eating: Eat one meal or snack per day with full attention. Notice colors, smells, textures, and flavors. Eat slowly, without screens or distractions. This can improve your relationship with food and increase satisfaction.

Building a Self-Care Routine

Physical Self-Care: Regular exercise (even 20-30 minutes daily makes a difference); Consistent sleep schedule (same bedtime and wake time); Nutritious meals and adequate hydration; Regular medical and dental checkups.

Emotional Self-Care: Journaling to process feelings; Talking to trusted friends or a counselor; Setting healthy boundaries in relationships; Allowing yourself to feel emotions without judgment; Practicing self-compassion.

Social Self-Care: Nurturing meaningful relationships; Making time for activities you enjoy with others; Limiting toxic or draining relationships; Participating in community or groups that share your interests.

Mental/Intellectual Self-Care: Reading for pleasure; Learning new skills; Taking breaks from studying to let your brain recover; Limiting excessive social media and screen time.

Spiritual/Values Self-Care: Connecting with your personal values; Engaging in practices that provide meaning (religion, philosophy, art, nature); Volunteering or contributing to causes you care about.

Creating Sustainable Self-Care Habits
Start small – choose one or two practices and do them consistently before adding more. Schedule self-care like you would any important appointment. Be flexible – what works during exams may differ from summer break. Self-care is not selfish; it's essential for sustained performance and wellbeing.

The Importance of Rest
Rest is not laziness – it's essential for cognitive function, emotional regulation, and physical health. Types of rest include physical rest (sleep and relaxation), mental rest (breaks from cognitive tasks), emotional rest (time away from emotional labor), social rest (solitude or time with low-demand companions), and creative rest (exposure to beauty and inspiration).

If you're struggling to maintain basic self-care, please speak with a counselor or mental health professional. Sometimes what we need most is support from others.
