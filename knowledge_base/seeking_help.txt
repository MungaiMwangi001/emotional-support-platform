When and How to Seek Professional Help

Seeking help for mental health challenges is a sign of strength and self-awareness, not weakness. Knowing when and how to access support can make a significant difference in your wellbeing.

When to Seek Professional Help
Consider reaching out to a mental health professional when:
- Emotional difficulties persist for more than two weeks and don't improve with self-care
- Your functioning at school, work, or in relationships is significantly impacted
- You're using alcohol or substances to cope
- You're experiencing persistent physical symptoms without medical cause
- You're having thoughts of harming yourself or others
- You feel unable to manage daily activities
- Family or friends have expressed concern about your wellbeing

Types of Mental Health Support

Campus Counseling Centers
Most universities offer free or low-cost counseling services to enrolled students. Services typically include individual therapy, group therapy, workshops, and crisis support. Wait times vary; if you're struggling, reach out early.

Community Mental Health Resources
If campus services aren't available or have long wait times, community mental health centers, private therapists, and online therapy platforms can provide support.

Crisis Support
If you're in immediate danger or crisis:
- Call or text 988 (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline in the US)
- Text HOME to 741741 (Crisis Text Line)
- Call emergency services (911 in the US)
- Go to your nearest emergency room

Telehealth Options
Many therapists now offer video sessions, making mental health support more accessible for students with busy schedules or transportation challenges.

Preparing for Your First Appointment
It's normal to feel nervous. You can share as much or as little as you're comfortable with. You might describe how long you've been struggling, how it's affecting your life, and what kind of help you're hoping for. If the first therapist isn't a good fit, it's okay to try someone else.

Supporting Others
If you're concerned about a friend, express your concern directly and compassionately ("I've noticed you seem down lately and I care about you. Are you okay?"). Listen without judgment. Encourage them to seek professional help and offer to help them find resources if needed.
