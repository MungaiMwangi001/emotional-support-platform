Understanding and Managing Anxiety

Anxiety is the most common mental health concern among college students. While some anxiety is normal and even helpful, excessive anxiety can interfere with daily life and academic performance.

What is Anxiety?
Anxiety is a feeling of worry, nervousness, or unease about an event or situation with an uncertain outcome. It involves physical symptoms like racing heart, sweating, trembling, and shortness of breath, as well as psychological symptoms like excessive worry and difficulty concentrating.

Types of Anxiety Students Commonly Experience
- Test anxiety: Fear and apprehension specific to examinations
- Social anxiety: Fear of social situations and negative evaluation by others
- Generalized anxiety: Persistent, excessive worry about various aspects of life
- Presentation anxiety: Fear of public speaking or presentations

Evidence-Based Strategies for Managing Anxiety

Cognitive Techniques
Challenge anxious thoughts by asking: "Is this thought based on facts or assumptions?" "What's the most realistic outcome?" "Have I survived similar situations before?" "What would I tell a friend in this situation?"

Behavioral Strategies
Gradual exposure to anxiety-provoking situations can reduce their power over time. Avoidance reinforces anxiety. Breaking feared situations into smaller steps and approaching them systematically builds confidence.

Grounding Techniques
The 5-4-3-2-1 technique: Name 5 things you can see, 4 things you can touch, 3 things you can hear, 2 things you can smell, 1 thing you can taste. This brings you back to the present moment when anxiety pulls you toward future worries.

Lifestyle Factors
Adequate sleep (7-9 hours), regular exercise, limiting caffeine and alcohol, and maintaining social connections all significantly affect anxiety levels.

Seeking Help
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is the most evidence-based treatment for anxiety disorders. Many university counseling centers offer CBT or can refer you to appropriate resources. Medication may also be helpful in combination with therapy for some individuals.
